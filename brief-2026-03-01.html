Why Children Need to Be Included in Discussions About AI
A WEF essay making the case that children are the largest group of humans who will spend their entire adult lives shaped by AI decisions made today — without their input. The argument isn't about child-proofing AI but about recognising children as stakeholders with distinct perspectives on the world they're being handed. AI and Education: Why Human Connection and Care Are Key
A pushback on the assumption that AI in education is primarily about personalisation and efficiency. The author argues that what schools do which AI cannot is create genuine conditions for care — and that optimising around AI's strengths may systematically erode the most irreplaceable thing education provides. A useful counterweight to default EdTech enthusiasm, and directly relevant to the question of what Codebloom is actually for.

What the Metaphor of 'Rewiring' Gets Wrong About Neuroplasticity
The brain-as-circuit metaphor has become so pervasive that we mistake it for description. This Aeon essay dismantles it carefully — tracing where the metaphor illuminates, where it distorts, and why the distortions matter for how we think about learning, habit change, and the kind of identity that practice shapes. The alternative isn't a better metaphor but a more honest reckoning with what neuroplasticity actually is and isn't.

The Japanese Ethics of 'Ningen' Dethrones the Western Self
Ningen (人間) translates roughly as "human" but more precisely as "the being between beings" — the self as constituted by relation rather than existing prior to it. This essay unpacks the ethical and philosophical implications: if identity is relational rather than bounded, the Western tradition's focus on individual rights and autonomous choice may be solving the wrong problem. A quiet but significant challenge to the assumptions underneath most Western philosophy of mind.
