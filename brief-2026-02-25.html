What Leadership Looks Like in an Agentic AI World
HBS Working Knowledge examines what changes when AI agents take on multi-step work rather than just answering questions. The argument centres on delegation: the skills needed to hand off outcomes to an agent — specifying goals clearly, knowing when to intervene, owning the result — turn out to look more like management than prompting. A reframe of the human-AI relationship that has concrete implications for how anyone works with these tools.

Teaching Children to Think with AI. And Learn the Skills AI Can't Replace.
Published last week, this piece from Nord Anglia's education network argues that AI literacy for children shouldn't start with generative tools — it should start with the habits that make AI meaningful to use: questioning, verifying, scepticism. The framing is precise: children who thrive won't be those who use AI most, but those who use it well. The harder question — what those skills actually are and how to build them in a classroom — is where the piece does its best work.

Imagination Isn't the Icing on the Cake of Human Cognition
If imagination is both our most distinctive cognitive faculty and one of the least studied, that asymmetry deserves more attention than it gets. This Aeon essay makes the case that imagination — not language, not reasoning — may be the most authentically human capacity, and that its near-invisibility in cognitive science is a revealing gap. The more we understand what machines cannot do, the sharper this argument becomes.

Why Everything You Know Might Be Wrong: Duality, Nonduality and Integration
A pointed argument: mainstream psychology has largely excluded nonduality from its models, treating it as fringe or spiritual, when it may be fundamental to how consciousness actually works. The author traces how Western mindfulness research systematically stripped the nondual dimension from the traditions it borrowed from — and what that omission costs both the science and the practice. A useful companion to anyone coming at this from the inside.

The Discovery of Aeonophiles Expands Our Definition of Life
Deep beneath the ocean floor lives a class of microbes that survive on 0.00001% of the energy required for any other known form of cell life — growing so slowly they can barely be said to grow at all. A December 2025 Aeon essay asks what they reveal about the definition of life itself. The answer is stranger than expected: aliveness, it turns out, may be a spectrum rather than a threshold.
